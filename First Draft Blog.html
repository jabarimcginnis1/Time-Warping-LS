Introduction

A common dilemma within neuroscience is correlating the patterns observed in neural data to a biological or behavioral observations. Since this process is often unsupervised and subjective, it is often challenging to determine whether neurons encode sensations or behavioral actions via their firing rates or temporal arrival of neural events. This ambiguity makes approaching analysis challenging. Especially regarding temporal variability within neural activity.

Temporal variability can be ambiguous at times due to uncertainty in our measurement tools. At times, it is hard to convince ourselves that what we are measuring is due to something biological or experimental. When observing something like a noisy neural raster, it is possible that inconcistencioes in timing alignment could be due to behavioral variablility, sensory delay, or intrinic noise within circuits. This temporal jitter can make trial-averaged data seem structureless or dampened event triggered averages. This is particularly tricky for analyses like PCA or regression because these types of analyses assume temporal consistency.

A solution to this problem was presented in Williams et al. (2020) where the authors proposed a solution rooted in least squares optimization: a method for time warping neural activity. The goal of this method was to reveal underlying struture in neural data by minimizing the temporal constraint on their experimental analyses. By learning per-trial time shifts or warping paths that align each observation to a shared latent trajectory, they were able to improve interpretability and signal-to-noise ratios of their datasets.

In this blog, I aim to walk through through some of the math and intuition behind this approach by walkin through some notation and applications. My aim is to try to show how a simple least sqaures formulation can expose temporal dynamics that would otherwise be lost in noise.

This technique is not only theoretically elegant, but is practical in visualizing and intepreting high dimensional datasets.

Notation

  • $X \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times N}$:observed neural data matrix (T timepoints, N Neurons)
  • $Y \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times N}$:latent shared structure
  • $W_i \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times T}$: warping matrix applied to trial $i$
  • $\hat{X}_i = W_i Y$: predicted/warped reconstruction of trial $i$
  • $\| A \|_F$: Frobenius norm
  • $L$: smoothness regularizer matrix (used to penalize rougness in warp paths)

This notation is the basis of the model. Trial-to-trial variability in timing is treated as a flexible source of variability that can be modeled. $W_i$ and $Y$ is optimized to minimize the reconstruction error $ | X_i - W_i Y \|_F^2$, which can also be subject to regularization constraint on $W_i$. These definitions also clarify how warping is interpreted not as a discrete shift but as a continuous, differentiable transformaion that maps time to time.

Why is this important?

It is important because of the mathematical nature of $W_i$. Unlike classical time-warping methods like dynamic time warping, which shifts or reorders points in discrete steps, the least squares framework defines $W_i$ using interpolation matrices. This means that these matrices can be parameterized continuously and optimized via gradients. Thus, it is differentiable... and a smooth remapping.

What is known?

Aligning data, particularly neural data by trials is a longstanding issue in neuroscience. From early single-unit studiest to modern imaging datasets, researchers have observed that neuronal responses vary in latency. Even in tightly controlled experiments, trial-to-trial shifts can distort the underlying response profile.

Standard dimensionality reduction techniques-such as PCA, t-SNE, and factor analysis operate under the assumption that trial structure is consistent across time. In practice, even small temporal jitters can inflate variance and lead to diffuse or uninterpretable components.

Time warping techniques were historically intrduced in fields like speech recognition (e.g. Dynamic Time Warping), but their non-differentiable nature made them unsuitable for modern optimization. Recasting the problem as a smooth, differentiable problem using least squares loss allowed for the problem to be solved by gradient-based solvers.

# Re-import necessary libraries after kernel reset
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from scipy.interpolate import interp1d
from scipy.optimize import minimize

# Set random seed and constants
np.random.seed(0)
T = 100
n_trials = 5
true_peak = 40
jitter_range = 10

# Generate synthetic trials with jittered peaks
signals = []
for _ in range(n_trials):
    jitter = np.random.randint(-jitter_range, jitter_range)
    signal = np.zeros(T)
    signal[true_peak + jitter] = 1
    signal = np.convolve(signal, np.hanning(10), mode='same')
    signal += np.random.normal(0, 0.05, size=T)
    signals.append(signal)
signals = np.array(signals)

# Define loss function for least squares alignment
def warp_loss(shift, ref, trial):
    x = np.arange(T)
    f = interp1d(x, trial, kind='linear', fill_value='extrapolate')
    x_shifted = x + shift[0]
    warped = f(x_shifted)
    return np.sum((ref - warped)**2)

# Use the mean of all trials as the reference
reference = np.mean(signals, axis=0)
aligned_signals = []
shifts = []

for trial in signals:
    result = minimize(warp_loss, [0.0], args=(reference, trial), method='Powell')
    optimal_shift = result.x[0]
    shifts.append(optimal_shift)
    f = interp1d(np.arange(T), trial, kind='linear', fill_value='extrapolate')
    aligned = f(np.arange(T) + optimal_shift)
    aligned_signals.append(aligned)

aligned_signals = np.array(aligned_signals)

# Compute averages
raw_average = np.mean(signals, axis=0)
aligned_average = np.mean(aligned_signals, axis=0)

# Plot both raw and aligned averages with overlays
fig, axs = plt.subplots(2, 1, figsize=(12, 8), sharex=True)

# Top: Raw signals
for i in range(n_trials):
    axs[0].plot(signals[i], alpha=0.4)
axs[0].plot(raw_average, color='red', linewidth=2, label='Raw Average')
axs[0].set_title('Figure 2 (Top): Raw Trials and Average (Before Alignment)')
axs[0].set_ylabel('Signal')
axs[0].legend()

# Bottom: Aligned signals
for i in range(n_trials):
    axs[1].plot(aligned_signals[i], alpha=0.4)
axs[1].plot(aligned_average, color='green', linewidth=2, label='Aligned Average (Least Squares)')
axs[1].set_title('Figure 2 (Bottom): Aligned Trials and Average (After Alignment)')
axs[1].set_xlabel('Time')
axs[1].set_ylabel('Signal')
axs[1].legend()

plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Solutions and Optimization

The core optimization is known as alternating least squares. The optimization alternates between solving for the shared trajectory Y an dthe warping paths $W_i$. This is feasible because the objective is quadratic in Y for a fixed $W_i$, and smooth in $W_i$ for a fixed Y.

While the overall approach relies on alternaing optimzation, Williams et al. (2020) used a brute-force search over potential time shifts for eacg trial to identify the optimal alignment parameters. This method, although computationally intensive, ensures convergence to low-error solutions and sevres as a practical workaround when gradient based methods are intractable.

But in the case that gradient based methods work, below I describe the steps of continuing with an alternating minimization strategy. This strategy breakys up the complex optimization problem into tractable subproblems, making it easier to regularize each step and interpret results.

Step 1: Fix $W_i$ and solve for $Y$ Close-form least squares: $Y = \left( \sum_i W_i^T W_i \right)^{-1} \left( \sum_i W_i^T X_i \right)$

Step 2: Fix Y, optimize $W_i$ requires solving a constrained optimization problem with gradient based methods.

Step 3: Alternate until you converge on a small number of iterations. This step can be done empirically.

Generalizations and Applications

Below I bulleted some examples of datasets and conditions that least-squares-based time warping can be applied to.

  1. Calcium imaging: By aligning trials with a variable onset latence, activity motifs can be recovered. (Pellegrino et al, 2024)

  2. Electrophysiology (neural spiking): Reveals a consistent structure from jittered spike rasters (Stine et al. 2023)

  3. fMRI: Compensate for subject-wise HRF delays across runs. May be tricky if trials are not seperated enough in time

  4. Optogenetics: In the cases where you may not be studying direct projections from chrimson expressing neurons, you can retroactively align behavioral or neural response latencies to stimulation.

  5. Reinforcement learning tasks: Recent evidence suggests that dopamine may reinforce behaviors independent of reward. Therfore it is plausible to align internal signals across training curves.

  6. Decision making tasks: Time warping can help compare neural dynamics across trials with different reaction times.

  7. Sleep studies/Memory: Align EEG/LFP recordings across sleep/oscillitory transitions to study the structure of spindles or other oscillatory patterns

  8. Manifold Learning: It is plausible to look at how neural actvity's trajectories change across embeddings by realigning data before further dimensionaliy reduction methods like PCA or T-SNE

  9. Neural Prosthetics/BCI: Warping has been shown to stabilize decoding for patterns like hand-writing/language

  10. Bayesian extension: It is plausible that warping can be used to treat $W_i$ as a latent with prior distributions.

Additional Information

Below I listed some additional things to not about this analysis method

  1. This model assumes gaussian noise, and for much of neuroscience it is standard to assume gaussian noise. Noise in biological systems are generally thought to arise many independent sources.

  2. The regularization parameter ($\lambda$) balances the trade off of fit versus smoothness. Therfore, cross-validation is an important step to add.

  3. Empirical understanding of experimental design is critical for interpreting results of the model. Warping does not enforce causality. Without constraints, it could create biologically implausible time reverals or anticipatory signals.

  4. As mentioned above, doing time warping on datasets before other dimensionality reduction methods like PCA and NMF can reveal underlying structure by removing temporal jitter. It is not always guaranteed, but visualizing your data beforehand may help you determine whether time warping is necessary.

  5. There is readily available code to implement time warping available onine. Implementations exist both in MATLAB or Python. I believe it is also possible to incorporate with PyTorch or TensorFlow to scale to larger datasets. https://github.com/ahwillia/affinewarp

  6. This method can be computationally expensive if you have many trials. Be sure to initialize your code well and prune search space where possible.

  7. To avoid overfitting or creating artifacts, be sure to visualize your warp paths $W_i$. Smooth and monotonic paths are preferred as shown below from Alex William's github. Link above.

  8. It is plausible to potentially combine time warping with other behavioral variables to leverage joint embedding analysis pipelines. (Schneider et al. 2023)

  9. Similarly to the previous bullet, time warping could also be combined with other dynamic latent models like LFADS for decoding stability (Pandarinath et al. 2018)

Together, these insights emphasize how least squares time warping serves as a modeling tool AND a diagnostic tool for potential trial structure and variability.

TL:DR

  • Neural dynamics vary in time across trials.
  • Time warping is a tool to align trials to expose potential shared latent structure.
  • Least squares serves as a tractable and interpretable solution to the problem.
  • Regularization helps prevent overfitting and ensures biological plausibility.
  • Can be applied to most of datasets generated in neuroscience (e.g. calcium imaging, electrophysiology, fMRI, etc.)

In summary, this blog post was inspired by Williams et al. 2020, to present a summary of time warping applications in neuroscience, particularly with an emphasis on the least-squars-based framework. This method is simple, scalable, and as aforementioned, applicable to most datasetes generated within neuroscience. Misalignment becomes a learnable parameter, and offers a statistical way to model neural dynamics with reduced temporal noise or variance.
